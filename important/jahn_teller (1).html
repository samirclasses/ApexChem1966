Crystal Field Theory · Distortion

A symmetric octahedron cannot stay symmetric.

When the d-orbitals of a transition metal ion are unevenly filled, the surrounding ligands shift to relieve the imbalance — stretching two bonds long, or squeezing them short. That's the Jahn–Teller effect.

Axial bonds stretch; equatorial bonds stay short.
Why it happens

Unequal electron occupancy breaks the symmetry

In an octahedral field, the five d-orbitals split into two sets: the lower t₂g (three orbitals) and the higher eg (two orbitals). If the eg set is occupied unevenly — one orbital with more electrons than the other — the ligand-electron repulsion along one axis differs from the others. The molecule distorts to lower its total energy.

Octahedral, undistorted
eg (dz², dx²−y²) — degenerate
t₂g (dxy, dxz, dyz) — degenerate
Jahn–Teller distorted (e.g. Cu²⁺, d⁹)
dx²−y² — higher, more occupied
dz² — lower, less occupied
dxz, dyz — pair
dxy — singlet

The eg set splitting is what matters most, since these orbitals point directly at the ligands. Splitting the t₂g set gives a much smaller energy gain because those orbitals point between ligands, not at them — that's why Jahn–Teller distortions are strongest when the eg set is asymmetrically filled (high-spin d⁴, d⁷, and especially d⁹).

Worked examples

Which ions distort, and how strongly

The strength of the effect depends on which d-orbital set is unevenly filled. eg-asymmetry (strong) bends bond lengths far more than t₂g-asymmetry (weak).

[Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺
d⁹ — eg unevenly filled

The textbook case. Two axial Cu–O bonds stretch to about 2.38 Å while the four equatorial bonds stay near 1.95 Å — a strong tetragonal elongation.

[Mn(H₂O)₆]³⁺
high-spin d⁴ — eg unevenly filled

One electron sits alone in the eg set, giving a strong elongation similar in magnitude to the d⁹ case — this is why Mn³⁺ complexes are often unstable and disproportionate.

[Ti(H₂O)₆]³⁺
d¹ — t₂g unevenly filled

The single electron sits in the lower t₂g set, which points between ligands. The resulting distortion is real but weak, and barely detectable in bond lengths.

[Ni(H₂O)₆]²⁺
d⁸ — eg evenly filled

Both eg orbitals hold one electron each (or both are filled) — perfectly symmetric occupancy, so no Jahn–Teller distortion is expected or observed.

[Fe(H₂O)₆]²⁺
high-spin d⁶ — t₂g unevenly filled

Occupancy is uneven only within t₂g. As with Ti³⁺, this gives a weak, often hard-to-resolve distortion.

[CrF₆]³⁻
d³ — both sets evenly filled

One electron in each t₂g orbital and an empty eg set — fully symmetric. No distortion: Cr³⁺ octahedra are famously regular.
