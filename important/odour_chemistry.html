01 — Fundamentals

What makes a molecule smell?

An odourant is any volatile chemical compound that can be detected by the olfactory system. For a molecule to be perceived as a smell, it must satisfy two essential criteria: it must be volatile enough to become airborne at ambient temperature, and it must interact with olfactory receptor proteins in the nasal epithelium.

Volatility is closely related to molecular size and polarity. Most odourants are small organic molecules with molecular weights between 30 and 300 g/mol. Above roughly 300 Da, molecules tend not to evaporate readily at room temperature — which is why heavy waxes and polymers are generally odourless.

The relationship between molecular structure and perceived smell is one of the deepest puzzles in sensory science. Tiny structural differences — a single methyl group, a different stereoisomer — can produce radically different odours.

"Smell is a potent wizard that transports you across thousands of miles and all the years you have lived."

— Helen Keller

Key Physical Properties

The following molecular properties correlate strongly with odour character and intensity:

Property Relevance to Odour
Vapour pressure Determines how readily a substance becomes airborne; higher pressure = stronger smell
Molecular weight Lower MW → more volatile; most odourants are 30–300 g/mol
Functional groups Aldehydes, esters, thiols, amines — each class confers characteristic scent notes
Chirality Mirror-image isomers often smell completely different (e.g. R/S-carvone)
Polarity Influences solubility in mucus and receptor binding kinetics

02 — Olfactory Reception

From molecule to perception

The journey from airborne molecule to conscious perception involves a remarkable biological cascade. The human nose contains around 6 million olfactory receptor neurons, each expressing one type of olfactory receptor (OR) protein from the largest gene family in the mammalian genome — roughly 400 functional OR genes in humans, compared to over 1,000 in mice.

Combinatorial Coding

A single odourant molecule typically activates multiple receptor types, and a single receptor responds to multiple odourants. The brain interprets smell not from a single receptor firing, but from the unique pattern — or combinatorial code — across hundreds of receptor types.

This combinatorial strategy allows the human nose to discriminate over one trillion distinct odour mixtures, a staggering figure determined by Bushdid et al. in 2014. The brain essentially performs high-dimensional pattern recognition on chemical space.

Adaptation and Fatigue

Olfactory adaptation — the rapid diminishing of perceived intensity with continued exposure — occurs at multiple levels. At the receptor level, conformational changes and phosphorylation reduce sensitivity. At the neural level, habituation occurs in olfactory cortex circuits.

This explains why entering a bakery, you strongly smell fresh bread, but residents nearby no longer notice it. Olfactory fatigue is specific to the adapted scent; other smells remain detectable, reflecting the receptor specificity of the adaptation mechanism.

03 — Theories of Smell

How do molecules produce quality?

The central question of olfactory science remains incompletely resolved: exactly what features of a molecule determine its perceived smell? Three major theoretical frameworks have been proposed, each with evidence for and against.

Lock & Key

Shape Theory

The dominant mainstream theory holds that odour is determined by the three-dimensional shape of a molecule — its size, geometry, and functional groups — and how precisely it fits into the binding pocket of an olfactory receptor protein, much like a key in a lock.

Evidence: stereoisomers like R/S-carvone smell different (spearmint vs. caraway), consistent with shape selectivity.

Vibrational

Vibration Theory

Proposed by Luca Turin, this controversial theory suggests receptors detect the vibrational frequencies of chemical bonds via quantum electron tunnelling. Molecules with similar shapes but different bond vibrations (e.g. due to deuterium substitution) should smell different.

Debate: some deuterated compounds do smell different, but reproducibility and mechanism remain contested.

Pluralist

Multi-Feature Theory

A growing view holds that no single molecular property explains odour; instead, receptors respond to multiple chemical features simultaneously. Computational and machine learning approaches are mapping the multidimensional chemical space of odour perception with increasing accuracy.

A 2022 deep learning model predicted perceived odour quality from molecular structure with near-human accuracy.

04 — Key Molecules

Notable odourant molecules

A survey of some of the most chemically interesting odourants illustrates how diverse functional groups and structures give rise to the vast landscape of human smell perception.

🌿
Geosmin
C₁₂H₂₂O

A bicyclic alcohol produced by Streptomyces bacteria in soil. The smell of rain on dry earth (petrichor). Detectable at just 5 parts per trillion — one of the lowest thresholds known.

Earthy · Rain · Soil
🌹
Rose oxide
C₁₀H₁₈O

A cyclic ether found in rose oil and geranium. Its four stereoisomers — differing only in spatial arrangement — each smell quite different, from floral to green to metallic.

Floral · Rose · Green
🍋
Limonene
C₁₀H₁₆

A cyclic monoterpene found in citrus peel. R-(+)-limonene smells of orange; S-(−)-limonene smells of turpentine. A classic demonstration of chiral odour discrimination.

Citrus · Fresh · Clean
🌲
α-Pinene
C₁₀H₁₆

The most abundant terpene in nature, found in conifer resin. Responsible for the iconic smell of pine forests and turpentine. Also found in rosemary and eucalyptus.

Pine · Woody · Resinous
🧄
Allicin
C₆H₁₀OS₂

An organosulfur compound produced when garlic is crushed. Formed enzymatically from alliin by alliinase. The archetypal sulfur odourant — pungent at minute concentrations.

Garlic · Pungent · Sulfurous
🌸
Musk ambrette
C₁₄H₂₀N₂O₅

A nitro-musk found in ambrette seed, used in perfumery. Musks in general are large cyclic molecules that stimulate a subset of olfactory receptors associated with animal scents.

Musky · Animal · Warm
🐟
Trimethylamine
(CH₃)₃N

A small tertiary amine produced by bacterial decomposition of TMAO in fish. The characteristic smell of rotting fish and body odour. Detectable at sub-ppm concentrations.

Fishy · Ammonia · Decay
🍫
Vanillin
C₈H₈O₃

An aldehyde extracted from vanilla beans or synthesised from lignin. One of the most widely used flavour and fragrance compounds globally. Structure: a hydroxybenzaldehyde with a methoxy group.

Sweet · Vanilla · Warm

05 — Classification

The odour wheel and classification systems

The odour wheel, inspired by perfumery's fragrance wheel, organises smells into primary families based on shared molecular characteristics and perceptual similarities. Real-world odours often blend categories.

Floral

Terpene alcohols (linalool, geraniol), phenylethanol. Found in flowers — roses, jasmine, lavender.

Fruity / Sweet

Short-chain esters (ethyl acetate, isoamyl acetate). Volatile carbonyls give ripe fruit aromas.

Herbaceous / Green

C6 aldehydes and alcohols (hexanal, leaf aldehyde). Cut grass and crushed leaf aromas.

Musky / Animal

Large cyclic macrolides and nitromusks. Low volatility, lingering — act as fixatives in perfumery.

Marine / Fresh

Calone, some aliphatic aldehydes. Evoke oceanic and ozonic freshness — widely used in modern perfumery.

06 — Applications

Odour chemistry in the real world

Perfumery & Flavour

The fragrance and flavour industry (valued at over $50 billion globally) is built on odour chemistry. Perfumers use a vocabulary of over 3,000 synthetic and natural odourant materials, structuring compositions into top notes (volatile, immediate), heart notes (lasting 30–60 min), and base notes (slow-evaporating fixatives).

Synthetic musks, aldehydes, and modern "captive" molecules unavailable in nature have transformed perfumery since the 20th century.

Food Science

Maillard reaction products — pyrazines, furans, and sulphurous compounds formed when amino acids react with reducing sugars at high heat — account for roasted, baked, and barbecued aromas. Controlled fermentation chemistry produces the esters and acids that give wines, beers, and cheeses their complex bouquets.

Gas chromatography–olfactometry (GC-O) allows scientists to pinpoint which trace molecules in a mixture are perceptually important.

Environmental & Medical

Electronic noses — arrays of chemical sensors whose combined response pattern identifies odours — are used in food quality control, environmental pollution monitoring, and emerging medical diagnostics. Dogs trained to detect cancer-associated volatile organic compounds (VOCs) in breath or urine achieve remarkable sensitivity.

Trimethylaminuria (fish odour syndrome) and maple syrup urine disease are examples of metabolic conditions diagnosed partly by characteristic odours.
