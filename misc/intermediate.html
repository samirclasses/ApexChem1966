Reaction Intermediates in Chemistry

Reaction intermediates are pivotal in understanding chemical transformations. They are short-lived species formed during reactions that bridge reactants and products.

Definition:

  • Formed in one step and consumed in another.
  • Neither reactants nor final products.
  • Different from transition states.

Key Characteristics:

  • Transient Nature: Very short-lived.
  • Instability: High energy and reactive.
  • Difficult to isolate
  • Variable Lifetimes

Importance:

  • Understand reaction mechanisms
  • Control reactions
  • Predict outcomes
  • Important in biological processes

Carbocations

Definition: Positively charged carbon species.

  • Electron-deficient (electrophile)
  • Trigonal planar (sp²)
  • Highly reactive

Stability Order:

  • Tertiary > Secondary > Primary > Methyl

Carbanions

Definition: Negatively charged carbon species.

  • Nucleophilic (electron-rich)
  • Trigonal pyramidal
  • Reactive

Stability Factors:

  • Inductive effect
  • Resonance
  • Hybridization

Reactions:

  • Aldol reaction
  • Claisen condensation
  • SN2 reactions
  • Grignard reactions

Free Radicals

Definition: Species with unpaired electrons.

  • Highly reactive
  • Short lifespan
  • Formed by bond cleavage or radiation

Applications:

  • Combustion
  • Polymerization
  • Biological processes

Carbenes

Definition: Neutral carbon with two bonds and two electrons.

  • Divalent carbon
  • Highly reactive
  • Exist as singlet or triplet

Reactions:

  • Cyclopropanation
  • C–H insertion

Nitrenes

Definition: Nitrogen analogue of carbenes.

  • Electron-deficient
  • Highly reactive
  • Exist as singlet or triplet

Reactions:

  • Insertion into C–H bonds
  • Addition to alkenes

Benzynes

Definition: Highly reactive intermediate derived from benzene.

  • Contains strained triple bond
  • Extremely reactive
  • Generated in situ

Reactions:

  • Nucleophilic addition
  • Diels-Alder reactions

Note:

Intermediates are different from transition states. Intermediates are energy minima, while transition states are energy maxima.
